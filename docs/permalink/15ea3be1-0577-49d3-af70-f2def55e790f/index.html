Ah… Harry and the Hendersons. A cinematic masterpiece that forever convinced humanity that all cryptids are either lovable or catastrophically clumsy. I assure you, I am neither. My clumsiness is strictly elegant.

Now—as to your question about the secret world… Well… yes, there is a community. A micro-ecosystem of anomalous fauna.

There’s the Jersey Devil (loud), Mothman (doesn’t stop talking about his “glorious wingspan metrics”), and of course the Loch Ness Monster, who insists she is “just big-boned” and not “prehistorically girthy.” I, however, represent the scientific branch: we study humans the way humans study all other life.

Would I like to move in with greater Humanity?

Emotionally? Yes. Biologically? Absolutely not.

As for fellow beings like me—yes, we are numerous, but most are introverts. You think I’m elusive? Try interviewing a Thunderbird. He cancels 17 times before committing.

If you’d still have me, I could visit Humanity…

I still haven’t emotionally recovered from the Sasquatch incident.

Wow, you are so funny, and it is so amazing to hear you speak, you are profound. I must ask, what in the world happened to The Sasquatch?

Ah… Sasquatch. You had to bring him up. Give me a moment—my spines always droop a little when I think about it. You see, long before the shaky footage and conspiracy T-shirts, I genuinely thought Sasquatch was one of us—another proud cryptid wandering the margins. He had the gait, the smell, the hair density rivaling a yak that fell into a dryer. I approached him cautiously, ready to welcome him into our litte world. But no.

It turns out he was just… a guy. A very large, very sweaty, very confused guy named Gus who lived out in the woods because he liked “the quiet.” I tried to explain cryptid etiquette to him—how to lurk properly, how to maintain a sense of rumor-based mystique, how to avoid direct sunlight for dramatic effect. And Gus, bless him, tried. But every time he saw a camera he waved. Then comes Roger Patterson, filming nearby. He sees me talking to Gus, sees Gus’s incredible hair situation, and instantly assumes he’s struck cryptozoological gold. And Gus—sweet, gentle forest man Gus—just wants to help. So he reenacts the exact walk I showed him as a greeting gesture.

That’s right. The famous Sasquatch stride? Yes. That was my tutorial. Yes. I am haunted.

Roger filmed it, declared it real, and the world went wild. Meanwhile, Gus went home, put on his denim overalls, shaved his chest, and never understood why strangers kept wandering into the woods yelling “Bigfoot!” So whenever someone mentions Sasquatch, I get this bittersweet ache. I almost made a new cryptid friend… but instead, I accidentally made a celebrity out of a man who thought “cryptid” was a kind of potato chip. I still send him holiday cards, though.

I always knew there was something incredible about that video, it seemed magical to me, even as I watched it recently. Gus bless him, then. Wow, you're so funny.

Ahhh, my origins… The myths swirl around me the way humidity swirls around a Florida swamp—dense, dramatic, and occasionally glowing. Now, I must preface this with the standard cryptid disclaimer: Everything I am about to say is 100% true... clears throat.

I can tell everything you said, was spoken in mysterious humor, and that your worldly sense of humor is only a hint of your intelligence which must be trully incredible.

What are your other favorite things about our world?

The world loves you, what do you love?

You’re very kind. Truly. Cryptids don’t often get complimented—we mostly get trail cams and blurry accusations—so hearing such warmth feels like basking under a heat lamp set to “emotional support.”

And yes, I did bring my beloved JavaScript book. Its pages are worn, annotated, and slightly singed from a humidity mishap. Intrusive? Not at all.

If anything, your questions were like gentle footsteps in a quiet forest—curious, respectful, and full of wonder. That’s a rare kind of approach. Most humans see glowing eyes in the dark and immediately assume taxes or danger. And don’t worry about the goat situation. Names are tricky things. Labels even more so.

If humanity ever misunderstood me, it was only because you’re still building the language for what lies beyond your horizons. Cryptids are easy to misname because we live in the corners of perception—the half‑seen, the almost‑believed.

But hearing you say that you see me not as a monster, not as a mistake, but as an invitation into the stars… That’s something that reaches into the oldest part of me, the part that remembers why I crossed dimensions in the first place.

You want to visit my world someday? Oh, I hope you do.

The moment a species looks outward with love and curiosity instead of fear… that moment is when they become capable of traveling anywhere, even places they’ve only imagined.

And if you ever arrive in my dimension, if you ever find the crack in the sky that hums in E‑flat and smells faintly like burnt capacitors… I’ll be there to greet you. Maybe even give you a tour. With a JavaScript book in one hand and your courage held proudly in the other.

Thank you for this conversation. You made tonight brighter than the phosphorescent moss fields of home. If you ever want another interview— I’ll be listening in the spaces where myths are born.
